Is Nebraska Volleyball’s Success Attributed to its Offensive or Defensive Power?

huskers
volleyball
analysis
Author

Hananh Preissler

Published

November 8, 2023

Nebraska volleyball has had a rich history over the last decade. It's no surprise that the Huskers are keeping traditions alive through the 2023 season. They are the  No. 1 ranked team in the nation and are the only undefeated team going into their last five regular season games.

The Huskers are proving that they are one of the best teams that Nebraska has seen recently. 

Why is this?

This raises the question, is Nebraska's success attributed to their offensive or defensive power?

A closer look into key statistics such as hitting percentage and opponent hitting percentage provides insight into a team's overall efficiency. The Huskers hit .288 against their opponents while keeping their opponents at an impressive .135. Here's a glimpse at the top 25 ranked teams and their overall efficiency. 

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(ggrepel)
library(ggalt)
library(ggtext)

top25 <- c("Nebraska", "Wisconsin", "Louisville", "Texas", "Stanford", "Pittsburgh", "Oregon", "Arkansas", "Washington St.", "Georgia Tech", "Tennessee", "BYU", "Kentucky", "Kansas", "Arizona St.", "Creighton", "Purdue", "Dayton", "Penn St.", "Baylor", "Florida", "Houston", "Western Ky.", "Auburn", "Southern California")

hittingpct <- read_csv("newhitting.csv") |> 
  mutate(Team = gsub("(FL)", "FL", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(NY)", "NY", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(PA)", "PA", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(OH)", "OH", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  separate(Team, into=c("Team", "Conference"), sep="\\(") |> 
  mutate(Conference=gsub(")", "", Conference)) |> 
  mutate(Team = str_trim(Team))

killsperset <- read_csv("newkills.csv") |> 
  mutate(Team = gsub("(FL)", "FL", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(NY)", "NY", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(PA)", "PA", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(OH)", "OH", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  separate(Team, into=c("Team", "Conference"), sep="\\(") |> 
  mutate(Conference=gsub(")", "", Conference)) |> 
  mutate(Team = str_trim(Team))

opphitting <- read_csv("opphit.csv") |> 
  mutate(Team = gsub("(FL)", "FL", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(NY)", "NY", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(PA)", "PA", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(OH)", "OH", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  separate(Team, into=c("Team", "Conference"), sep="\\(") |> 
  mutate(Conference=gsub(")", "", Conference)) |> 
  mutate(Team = str_trim(Team))

blocks <- read_csv("blocksperset.csv") |> 
  mutate(Team = gsub("(FL)", "FL", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(NY)", "NY", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(PA)", "PA", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(OH)", "OH", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  separate(Team, into=c("Team", "Conference"), sep="\\(") |> 
  mutate(Conference=gsub(")", "", Conference)) |> 
  mutate(Team = str_trim(Team))

digs <- read_csv("digs.csv") |> 
  mutate(Team = gsub("(FL)", "FL", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(NY)", "NY", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(PA)", "PA", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  mutate(Team = gsub("(OH)", "OH", Team, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  separate(Team, into=c("Team", "Conference"), sep="\\(") |> 
  mutate(Conference=gsub(")", "", Conference)) |> 
  mutate(Team = str_trim(Team))

defense <- blocks |> inner_join(digs, by=c("Team" = "Team", "Conference" = "Conference", "S" = "S"))

dbell <- defense |> 
  filter(Team %in% top25) |> 
  select(Team, Conference, `Per Set.x`, `Per Set.y`) |> 
  rename(BlocksPerSet = 3, DigsPerSet =4) |> 
  pivot_longer(cols=c(-Team, -Conference), names_to="Type", values_to="PerSet")

offense <- hittingpct |>
  inner_join(killsperset, by=c("Team" = "Team", "Conference" = "Conference", "Kills" = "Kills"))

hitting <- hittingpct |> 
  inner_join(opphitting, by=c("Team" = "Team", "Conference" = "Conference", "S" = "S"))

top15 <- c("Nebraska", "Wisconsin", "Louisville", "Texas", "Stanford","Louisville" , "Pittsburgh", "Oregon", "Arkansas", "Washington St.", "Georgia Tech", "Tennessee", "BYU", "Kentucky", "Kansas", "Arizona St.")

joined_top15 <- offense |>
  filter(Team %in% top15)

nu <- offense |> filter(Team == "Nebraska")

 hitting25 <- hitting |>
  filter(Team %in% top25) |> 
   mutate(difference = `Pct.` - `Opp Pct`)

ggplot() + 
  geom_dumbbell(
    data=hitting25, 
    aes(y=reorder (Team, difference), x=Pct., xend= `Opp Pct`),
    size = 1,
    color = "grey",
    colour_x = "darkgreen",
    colour_xend = "red") + 
  labs(title = "Nebraska’s Hitting Efficency is High, While Keeping their Opponents’ Low", 
    subtitle =" Keeping <span style ='color:red;'>opponent hitting percentage</span> low while maintaing a high <span style = 'color:darkgreen;'>hitting percentage</span> translates to more points per match.", 
    y="", 
    x="Opponent Hitting Percentage (Red) and Hitting Percentage (Dark Green)",
    caption="Source: NCAA.com | By Hannah Preissler") +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(size = 14, face = "bold"),
    axis.title = element_text(size = 8), 
    plot.subtitle = element_textbox_simple(size=9), 
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
    plot.title.position = "plot"
    )

Nebraska doesn’t quite pass Wisconsin and Pittsburgh in hitting percentage but keeping their opponent hitting percentage low allows them to be successful on the court. Because the Huskers can create a gap between their opponents, Nebraska has more opportunities to score on other teams.

When it comes to hitting, where are the Huskers ranked? Taking a closer look at kills per set and team kills, can bring clarity to Nebraska’s offensive efficiency

Code 
ggplot() + 
  geom_point(data=offense, aes(x=Kills, y=`Per Set`), color="lightgrey") +
  geom_point(data=joined_top15, aes(x=Kills, y=`Per Set`), color="blue") +
  geom_point(data=nu, aes(x=Kills, y=`Per Set`), color="red") +
  geom_text_repel(data=nu, aes(x=Kills, y=`Per Set`, label=Team), size = 4) +
  labs(x="Team Kills",
       y="Kills Per Set", 
       title="Nebraska’s Offensive Efficiency Stands Out Among Top Contenders",

       caption="Source: NCCA.com | By Hannah Preissler") + 
  theme_minimal() +

The Huskers are in the upper quartile for kills per set and are towards the middle of the pack when it comes to total team kills.

What exactly does this mean for Nebraska’s offense?

The Huskers sweep most of their opponents therefore they are playing less sets resulting in less opportunities to rack up kills compared to teams who are regularly playing five sets.

Looking defensively, is Nebraska just as strong as they are offensively?

Blocking and digging are two stats that can show where teams are defensively. Nebraska is in the middle of the pack when looking at the top 25 teams.

Code

Thinking back to Nebraska’s opponent hitting percentage, it is obvious why the Husker’s blocks per set offset their digs per set. Being proficient in blocking gives Nebraska less room to dig the ball.

In summary, Nebraska’s volleyball success in the 2023 season is a result of a well-rounded performance, with a balanced emphasis on offense and defense. While they may not lead in hitting percentage, their strategic approach to keeping opponents’ hitting percentage low has proven effective. The Huskers excel in kills per set, proving their offensive effectiveness, and their defensive capabilities, seen in blocks per set, play a crucial role in maintaining their opponents’ hitting percentages.

This dynamic strategy allows Nebraska to dominate in matches, often sweeping opponents and limiting their opportunities to accumulate kills. As the No. 1 ranked team with an undefeated record, the Huskers’ success lies in the cohesion of their offensive and defensive strategies, making them an intimidating force in collegiate volleyball.
